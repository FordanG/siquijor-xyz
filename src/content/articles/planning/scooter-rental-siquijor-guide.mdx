Siquijor is one of those rare Philippine islands where renting a scooter genuinely makes sense for almost every type of traveler. The island is compact, the circumferential road is well-maintained, and the pace of life means traffic is almost nonexistent. A scooter gives you freedom to explore hidden waterfalls, secluded beaches, and mountain viewpoints on your own schedule, without waiting for tricycles or negotiating tour prices.

This guide covers everything from where to rent, what to expect on the roads, and how to stay safe while riding around one of the Visayas' most beautiful islands.

## Why a Scooter Is the Best Way to Explore Siquijor

The entire island measures roughly 72 kilometers around its circumferential road. On a scooter, you can complete the full loop in about two hours without stopping, though you will want to stop constantly. The island rewards spontaneous detours: that unmarked trail to a swimming hole, the roadside sari-sari store with the best banana cue, the viewpoint where you can see Cebu and Negros on a clear day.

Tricycles are available but expensive for longer distances, and their routes are limited. Hiring a private van or joining a guided tour locks you into someone else's itinerary. A scooter puts you in control, and on an island this size, getting lost is part of the charm. You are never more than 20 minutes from the main road.

## Where to Rent a Scooter in Siquijor

### Resort and Hotel Rentals

Most accommodations in Siquijor either rent scooters directly or can arrange one through a local contact. This is the most convenient option because the scooter is delivered to your door, and your host serves as the point of contact if anything goes wrong.

Expect to pay between 350 and 500 PHP per day for a standard 110cc to 125cc automatic scooter through your accommodation. Some resorts charge a premium, so it helps to ask the price before committing.

### Rental Shops in San Juan and Siquijor Town

The highest concentration of rental shops sits along the main road in San Juan, the tourism hub of the island. Several shops near Paliton Beach Road and along the San Juan waterfront offer daily and weekly rates. Siquijor Town, near the port, also has options, especially convenient if you want to ride out as soon as you step off the ferry.

Typical daily rates at rental shops:

- 110cc automatic (Honda Click, Yamaha Mio): 300 to 400 PHP per day
- 125cc semi-automatic (Honda Wave, XRM): 250 to 350 PHP per day
- 150cc manual (Honda TMX, Yamaha Sniper): 400 to 500 PHP per day

Weekly rentals usually come with a discount, bringing the daily rate down by 50 to 100 PHP. Always negotiate for stays longer than three days.

### What You Need to Rent

Requirements vary by rental provider, but the standard expectations are:

- A valid driver's license (Philippine or international). Some shops accept any foreign license, while others specifically request an International Driving Permit (IDP). Bring your IDP to avoid complications.
- A passport or government ID left as deposit, or a cash deposit ranging from 1,000 to 3,000 PHP.
- Signing a basic rental agreement outlining liability for damage or loss.

Some shops are more relaxed about documentation than others, but having your IDP and a photocopy of your passport ready streamlines the process.

## Inspecting the Scooter Before You Ride

Take five minutes before riding off to check the basics. This protects you from being blamed for pre-existing damage and ensures your safety on the road.

Walk around the scooter and note any scratches, dents, or cracked mirrors. Take photos with your phone, including the odometer reading and fuel level. Check the brakes by squeezing both levers. Test the horn, headlight, turn signals, and brake lights. Look at the tires for adequate tread and proper inflation. Make sure the side stand and center stand both work.

Ask the rental provider for their contact number and confirm what happens if you get a flat tire or mechanical issue. Most will send someone to help, but you want that confirmed in advance.

## Road Conditions and What to Expect

### The Circumferential Road

The main ring road around Siquijor is paved and in generally good condition. It follows the coastline for most of the route, offering continuous ocean views. The road is two lanes wide, with occasional narrowing in more rural stretches. Surface quality ranges from smooth asphalt in town centers to patchy pavement with minor potholes in between. Nothing that would trouble even a cautious rider, but staying alert is important.

### Interior and Mountain Roads

Roads climbing toward the interior, particularly up to Mount Bandilaan, Cantabon Cave, and the various waterfall access points, are a different story. Many are concrete but steep, with sections that turn to gravel or packed earth, especially after rain. The road to Lugnason Falls and some sections around Lazi have steep grades that require confidence on a scooter.

If you are an inexperienced rider, stick to the circumferential road and take tricycles to interior attractions. The mountain roads are manageable for experienced riders but can be slippery when wet.

### Road Hazards

Dogs sleeping on warm asphalt are the most common hazard in Siquijor. Slow down through barangays and residential areas. Chickens, goats, and the occasional carabao also wander onto the road without warning.

Watch for sand patches where side roads meet the main road, especially near beaches. After rain, leaf litter and mud can make corners slippery. At night, the road is poorly lit outside of town centers, and pedestrians in dark clothing are difficult to see.

## Fuel Stations and Costs

Siquijor has a handful of proper gasoline stations, primarily in Siquijor Town and near San Juan. Several barangays along the circumferential road have informal fuel sellers: look for recycled soft drink bottles filled with gasoline displayed on wooden stands outside homes. These are common throughout the Philippines and perfectly fine to use.

A full tank on a 110cc scooter costs approximately 150 to 200 PHP and will last you a full day of exploring, easily covering 80 to 100 kilometers. Fuel prices on the island run slightly higher than mainland Negros or Cebu due to transport costs. As of early 2026, expect around 65 to 75 PHP per liter.

Fill up in town before heading to the interior. There are no fuel options on the mountain roads.

## Safety Tips for Riding in Siquijor

### Wear a Helmet

Every rental should include at least one helmet. Insist on it. Philippine law requires helmets, and while enforcement in Siquijor is relaxed, head injuries from low-speed falls are no joke. If the provided helmet is cracked, loose, or does not have a functional chin strap, ask for a replacement or bring your own.

### Ride Sober

The island vibe is relaxed, and it is easy to have a few drinks at a beach bar and hop on a scooter. Do not do it. The roads are poorly lit at night, dogs appear from nowhere, and even a minor fall on coral-embedded pavement causes serious skin damage. If you plan to drink, park the scooter and take a tricycle back.

### Drive on the Right, Overtake on the Left

The Philippines drives on the right side of the road. Overtaking is done on the left. Use your horn before blind corners, especially on narrow interior roads. Filipino drivers and fellow scooter riders generally ride predictably, but buses and trucks on the circumferential road can take wide turns.

### Watch Your Speed

There is no reason to speed in Siquijor. The island is small, the scenery is beautiful, and the road rewards a pace of 30 to 40 kilometers per hour. Through towns and barangays, keep it under 20. Speed bumps appear without warning, sometimes unmarked, and hitting one at speed is a guaranteed bad time.

### Rain Protocol

If rain starts while you are riding, slow down significantly and consider pulling over under a covered structure until it passes. Philippine rain can go from drizzle to downpour in seconds. The roads become slick, visibility drops, and stopping distances increase dramatically on scooter tires. Most rain passes within 30 to 60 minutes.

## Suggested Scooter Routes

### The Full Island Loop

Start from San Juan heading south along the coast. Pass through Lazi, admire the centuries-old church and convent, continue through Maria and Enrique Villanueva, then north through Larena and back to San Juan. The full loop takes about two hours of pure riding time, but plan a full day with stops.

Highlights along the route include Paliton Beach, Lazi Church, Kagusuan Beach, Salagdoong Beach, and the coastal viewpoints between Maria and Enrique Villanueva.

### San Juan to Cambugahay Falls

Head south from San Juan toward Lazi. Follow the signs to Cambugahay Falls, which sit just off the main road. The access road is paved and manageable. Park at the designated area and walk down the steps to the falls. Total ride time from San Juan is about 25 minutes.

### Mount Bandilaan Summit

From the circumferential road near Cantabon, follow the signs inland toward Mount Bandilaan. The climb is steady, with switchbacks and steep sections. The summit offers panoramic views of the island and the surrounding Visayan Sea. The road is concrete but narrow, with a few rough patches. Allow 30 to 40 minutes for the ascent from the main road.

### West Coast Sunset Run

Ride from San Juan north toward Larena in the late afternoon. The western coastline catches the sunset beautifully, with several pull-off points where you can watch the sun drop behind Negros. The stretch between Tubod and San Juan is particularly scenic. Time your departure so you are back before full dark.

## What to Do if Something Goes Wrong

### Flat Tire

Flat tires happen, especially on interior roads with loose gravel. Most barangays have a vulcanizing shop (tire repair) within walking distance. Look for signs saying "Vulcanizing" or ask any local. Repairs typically cost 30 to 50 PHP and take 15 minutes.

### Breakdown

Call your rental provider. Most will either send someone to fix the issue or deliver a replacement scooter. Keep their number saved in your phone and accessible offline.

### Accident

If you are involved in an accident, stay calm. Check for injuries first. For minor incidents, exchange information with the other party and contact your rental provider. For serious injuries, call emergency services or ask a local to help. The nearest hospital with emergency capability is in Siquijor Town.

Rental insurance is virtually nonexistent in Siquijor. You are typically liable for damage to the scooter. Travel insurance that covers motorcycle or scooter use is strongly recommended. Check your policy specifically for two-wheeled vehicle coverage, as many standard travel insurance plans exclude it.

## Alternatives to Scooter Rental

If riding a scooter is not for you, Siquijor still has workable transportation options.

**Tricycles** operate throughout the island and can be hired for point-to-point trips or half-day and full-day tours. A full-day tricycle tour covering major attractions costs between 1,500 and 2,500 PHP.

**Multicabs and jeepneys** run limited routes between major towns, primarily Siquijor Town to Larena, and Siquijor Town to San Juan. Departures are infrequent and schedules are loose.

**Private car hire** with a driver is available through some resorts and tour operators, typically costing 3,000 to 5,000 PHP for a full day.

**Bicycle rental** exists at a few spots in San Juan but is challenging given the heat and the hilly interior terrain. Best suited for short coastal rides only.

## Final Thoughts

Renting a scooter transforms a Siquijor trip from good to unforgettable. The island is built for two-wheeled exploration: small enough that you cannot truly get lost, beautiful enough that every wrong turn leads somewhere worth seeing, and quiet enough that the ride itself becomes part of the experience.

Take it slow, respect the road, and let the island surprise you. That unmarked turnoff probably leads to a waterfall. That narrow path through the coconut palms probably ends at a beach. In Siquijor, the best discoveries happen when you have the freedom to follow your curiosity, and a scooter gives you exactly that.
